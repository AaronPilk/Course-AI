# How to hire (or fire) an SEO without getting scammed

## The puzzle

A consultant emails you out of the blue. They've "noticed" your site isn't ranking well. They can guarantee page-1 placement in 30 days for $899/month. They have a special relationship with Google. They have hundreds of testimonials.

Is this a real opportunity or are you about to get scammed?

It's a scam, and recognizing it is one of the most valuable business skills you can develop. Google has published an entire document specifically to help you avoid SEO fraud. This lesson distills it.

## The simple version

Google's "Do you need an SEO?" doc has a section called *"Precautions"* that lists the red flags of a scammer:

- **Cold emails about your SEO** (out-of-the-blue outreach is almost never legitimate)
- **Guarantees of rankings** (impossible to honestly promise)
- **Claims of a "special relationship" with Google** (no such thing exists)
- **Won't explain what they'll do** (transparency is required)
- **"Link popularity schemes"** or buying links (spam policy violations)
- **Promises of "priority submit to Google"** (no such thing)
- **Hidden ownership of "shadow domains"** that can be redirected away
- **"Doorway pages"** stuffed with keywords pointing to your site

If a prospective SEO has any of these red flags, walk away. Don't argue. Don't negotiate. Walk.

## The technical version

### Red flags, expanded

**1. Unsolicited cold emails.**
From Google directly:
> "Be wary of SEO firms and web consultants or agencies that email you out of the blue."

Legitimate SEOs almost never cold-email random business owners. The unsolicited "we noticed your site needs help" email is the same playbook as the "your car warranty is expiring" robocall.

**2. Ranking guarantees.**
Google:
> "No one can guarantee a #1 ranking on Google. Beware of SEOs that claim to guarantee rankings, allege a 'special relationship' with Google, or advertise a 'priority submit' to Google."

There is no priority submission. There is no relationship. No human at Google can promise to rank you. Period.

A reputable SEO might say: "I'm confident we can improve your visibility for these queries over 6–12 months." That's an honest framing. "Guaranteed #1 in 30 days" is a sales lie.

**3. Won't explain what they'll do.**
Google:
> "Be careful if a company is secretive or won't clearly explain what they intend to do."

If they claim "proprietary techniques" they can't share — walk. Legitimate SEO work is mostly transparent: technical audits, content recommendations, monitoring. Anything "secret" is usually something Google would penalize you for.

**4. Link schemes and "link popularity" tactics.**
Buying links, joining link-exchange programs, using Private Blog Networks (PBNs), getting "link popularity" services — all violations of Google's spam policies. From the doc:
> "Avoid getting involved in link schemes, such as buying links from other sites to increase your ranking. This is against Google's spam policies and can result in a manual action against some or all of your site."

A consultant who recommends *any* paid link arrangement other than disclosed sponsorships is recommending a spam policy violation. Walk.

**5. Shadow domains.**
Google explains:
> "One common scam is the creation of 'shadow' domains that funnel users to a site by using deceptive redirects. These shadow domains are often owned by the SEO who claims to be working on a client's behalf. However, if the relationship sours, the SEO may point the domain to a different site, or even to a competitor's domain."

You pay an SEO. They register a bunch of domains with keywords in them. Those domains redirect to your site. You see "traffic increases." Then you fire them — and they redirect those domains away from you. Worse: those domains are sometimes used for other spam, dragging your reputation down.

If an SEO talks about "creating sites that funnel to yours," ask who owns the domains. If it's them, walk.

**6. Doorway pages.**
> "Another illicit practice is to place 'doorway' pages loaded with keywords on the client's site somewhere. The SEO promises this will make the page more relevant for more queries... More insidious, however, is that these doorway pages often contain hidden links to the SEO's other clients."

These are pages stuffed with keywords designed only to rank, often with hidden links pointing back to the SEO's other clients (so your domain authority gets siphoned to them). Always a spam policy violation.

**7. Hidden text and cloaking.**
"White-on-white" keyword stuffing, hidden divs full of keywords, showing different content to Googlebot than to users — all classic cloaking. Always spam violations.

### How to interview an SEO (Google's questions)

Google's doc includes a list of questions to ask. Here are the most important ones, with the answers a *good* SEO would give:

**Q: Can you show me examples of your previous work and success stories?**
Good answer: Yes, here are case studies with specific clients, specific queries, before/after metrics, and the work that drove the change. They explain causation clearly.
Bad answer: Vague claims, no specifics, lots of testimonials but no metrics.

**Q: Do you follow Google's Search Essentials?**
Good answer: Yes — explains what they are and how their work aligns. Specifically rejects link-buying, PBNs, doorway pages, cloaking.
Bad answer: Vague yes. Or they pivot to "well, you have to do *some* gray-hat stuff."

**Q: What kind of results do you expect, and in what timeframe?**
Good answer: Concrete but humble. "Based on your starting point, I'd expect [specific metric] to move by [specific amount] over [4–12 months]. There's variability based on competition and update cycles." They name *what* will improve, not just abstract "rankings."
Bad answer: Specific guarantees. Or vague "results within weeks."

**Q: What's your experience in my industry / country?**
Good answer: Honest specifics. "I've worked with two similar companies; here's what I learned." Or: "I haven't worked in your industry but here's what's transferable from this related area."
Bad answer: "I've worked with everyone; we're great at every niche."

**Q: How can I expect to communicate with you? Will you share all changes and reasoning?**
Good answer: Regular reporting cadence, named contacts, full visibility into changes and rationale.
Bad answer: "Don't worry about the details, we'll handle everything." Or no clear reporting structure.

### Questions the good SEO will ask *you*

This is at least as important. A real SEO consultant wants to understand your business before pitching tactics.

Questions they should ask:
- What makes your business or service unique and valuable to customers?
- Who are your customers?
- How does your business make money? How can search help?
- What other channels are you investing in?
- Who are your competitors?

If they don't ask these — and just pitch SEO services on autopilot — they're not the right hire.

### The audit step

A reputable SEO will offer a technical and content audit before committing to a long engagement. Google:
> "Ask for a technical and search audit for your site to learn what they think needs to be done, why, and what the expected outcome should be. You'll probably have to pay for this. You will probably have to give them read-only access to your site on Search Console."

Key elements of a good audit:
- Read-only (not write) access to Search Console at first
- Specific findings with severity and recommended fixes
- Realistic estimates of timeline and impact
- No outrageous "every page has critical errors" hyperbole
- No magical recommendations like "we need to install our proprietary tracking script"

The audit itself is a hiring decision — if the audit is shallow or full of myths, walk before you commit further.

### When to fire your current SEO

Signs you should fire your current SEO:

- They can't or won't explain what they're doing in any specific terms
- They've been working for 6+ months with no measurable progress on agreed-upon metrics
- They've recommended or implemented anything from the red-flag list
- They lock you in with proprietary tools you can't take away when you leave
- Communication is opaque, sporadic, or defensive
- They blame Google updates for every setback without owning their part
- They register domains "on your behalf" but won't transfer ownership
- The relationship feels off in your gut

How to fire safely:
- Make sure *you* own everything: domains, hosting, accounts, content. Get login credentials transferred *before* you give notice.
- Pull a complete export of analytics data, Search Console data, and any reports they've produced.
- Document the work they did so you can evaluate what to keep vs. revert.
- Don't burn the bridge publicly — Google does not retaliate but the SEO might.

### How to report fraud

If you've been defrauded by an SEO, Google's doc points to:

- **United States**: Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at <https://www.ftc.gov/> or 1-877-FTC-HELP
- **International**: <https://www.econsumer.gov/>

Report doesn't undo the damage but helps protect future businesses from the same actors.

## An analogy: hiring a contractor for home renovation

A contractor knocks on your door unsolicited. They tell you they noticed your roof needs work. They guarantee they can fix it for $899. They have a "special relationship" with the city inspector. They want a deposit today.

You'd refuse instantly. The patterns are obvious in physical services.

The same patterns in SEO get past people because the service is abstract. The cold email, the guarantee, the special relationship, the urgency, the secrecy — it's the same scam, dressed in digital marketing terminology.

When the patterns feel familiar, trust your instincts. The SEO industry has more fraud per capita than the home renovation industry. Apply the same skepticism.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The shadow-domain rug pull.**
A small business hired an SEO firm. The firm registered 30 "supporting" domains and pointed them all at the client's site. Traffic appeared to grow. After 12 months, the client tried to switch providers. The firm pointed all 30 domains at a competitor's site. Traffic collapsed. Recovery required legal action; the firm had buried domain ownership behind multiple layers. Lesson: *always* own your own domains.

**Scenario 2: The honest audit that saved a deal.**
A founder considered hiring an agency. The agency offered a paid technical audit first. The audit was thorough, identified 8 real issues, prioritized them by impact, and gave realistic timelines. The founder hired them. Two years later, the relationship is still working. Lesson: a real audit upfront screens for the right partners.

**Scenario 3: The 4-month no-results breakup.**
A company hired an SEO consultant on a 6-month retainer. By month 4, no movement on any agreed metric. When pressed, the consultant blamed "core update volatility" — but had no specific account of what they'd actually done. The company fired and brought the work in-house. Within 3 months of doing the basics themselves (Module 3 fundamentals) traffic was moving again.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Hiring on a cold pitch.** Almost guaranteed bad outcome.
- **Believing rank guarantees.** Impossible to honestly promise. Anyone who does is lying.
- **Letting the SEO register your domains.** Always own your domain registration yourself.
- **Letting the SEO have admin (write) access to Search Console / Analytics before you've vetted them.** Read-only first.
- **Signing 12-month contracts without performance milestones.** Shorter engagement with checkpoints is safer.
- **Not asking for specific case studies and references.** Real SEOs have them. Scammers don't.
- **Staying out of guilt or sunk cost.** Bad SEO costs you more by staying than firing.

## Summary

- The SEO industry has significant fraud. Recognize the patterns: cold emails, ranking guarantees, "special relationships," secrecy, link schemes, shadow domains, doorway pages.
- Interview prospective SEOs using Google's own questions. Make sure they ask *you* good business questions back.
- Always own your domains, accounts, and data. Never let an SEO register domains "on your behalf."
- Start with a paid technical audit before committing to long retainers.
- Fire SEOs who can't explain what they're doing, can't show measurable results after 6+ months, or recommend any spam-policy violation.
- If defrauded: report to FTC (US) or econsumer.gov (international).
