# Do you even need SEO? A decision framework

## The puzzle

A friend asks you for advice. They've launched a business. Should they hire an SEO?

You realize you don't actually know how to answer that. "SEO is good for everyone" sounds vacuous. "Most businesses waste their SEO budget" sounds cynical. The honest answer depends on the business — and most people skip directly to "yes I need SEO" without doing that math.

This lesson is the math. By the end, you'll know when SEO is the right priority, when it isn't, and how much to invest if it is.

## The simple version

Three questions decide whether SEO matters for your business:

1. **Do your customers use Google to find businesses like yours?**
   If they don't (e.g., you're a B2B sales-led company with no inbound funnel), SEO is at best a low priority.

2. **Can your business model afford the timeline?**
   SEO typically takes **4 months to a year** to show results. If you need revenue in 30 days, paid acquisition is the answer, not SEO.

3. **Are you willing to invest in the work?**
   SEO requires real content, real technical fixes, and real time. If you're not willing to commit, hiring an SEO consultant is wasted budget.

If you answer yes to all three, SEO probably belongs in your strategy. If you answer no to any, you have to think harder.

## The technical version

### Question 1: Customer behavior

Google's own framing, from the "Do you need an SEO?" doc:

> "If you run a small local business, you can probably do much of the work yourself."

The broader point: how customers find businesses like yours determines whether SEO is leverage or noise.

**SEO usually high-leverage for:**

- **Local businesses** where searches for "[service] near me" or "[service] in [city]" are how customers find you (restaurants, dentists, plumbers, lawyers, gyms).
- **Information businesses** (publishers, content marketers, SaaS with content-led growth).
- **E-commerce** with products people actually search for.
- **Specialty professional services** where searches for problems-you-solve drive leads.

**SEO usually low-leverage for:**

- **Pure outbound B2B sales** where deals come from cold outreach, not inbound queries.
- **Closed networks / community-driven businesses** where customers come from referrals.
- **Products customers don't search for** (genuinely novel categories, early-stage innovations).
- **Brand-driven businesses** where the brand name is the only relevant search term (and you already rank for it).

**Edge case: businesses where SEO matters less than you'd think.**

Many businesses *can* be discovered through search but most of their revenue comes from other channels. For these, SEO is a "should we invest some" question, not a "should we prioritize" question.

### Question 2: Timeline

Google says it directly:

> "It will take time for you to see results: typically from four months to a year from the time you begin making changes until you start to see the benefits."

This is the most under-appreciated reality of SEO. A business that needs revenue *now* shouldn't be its primary lever for *now*. SEO is a compounding investment, not a sprint.

If you need:

- **Revenue in 30–90 days** — paid ads, outbound sales, partnerships.
- **Revenue in 90–180 days** — paid + some quick-win SEO (Google Business Profile, basic technical fixes).
- **Revenue in 6+ months** — SEO becomes a real lever, especially compounded by other channels.
- **Sustainable acquisition over years** — SEO + content compound; this is where they earn their cost.

Pairing matters. A common winning pattern: run paid acquisition to learn what messaging and queries convert, *then* invest in SEO to capture those same queries cheaply over the long term.

### Question 3: Commitment

SEO that doesn't get implemented doesn't work. Auditors and consultants regularly hand clients 60-page reports that get filed in a Drive folder and never acted on. Spending $5,000 on an unimplemented audit is worse than spending $0.

Google's framing:

> "Be committed to implementing the recommended changes. Making the changes recommended by an SEO takes time and effort; if you aren't going to take the time to make these changes, it's not worthwhile hiring a professional."

If you're going to hire an SEO, you (or your team) need to:

- Be able to actually publish content (writers, editors, a content workflow)
- Have someone who can make technical changes to the site
- Be willing to act on recommendations even when they're inconvenient (rewriting underperforming pages, killing pages, restructuring)
- Hold the timeline (give the work 6+ months to compound)

If those aren't true, fix the prerequisites first, then hire the SEO.

### A practical decision matrix

For your business, score each:

| Factor | Score |
|---|---|
| Customers search Google for what you offer | 0 (rarely) to 3 (always) |
| You can afford 4–12 months until results | 0 (no) to 3 (yes, planning for it) |
| You can commit to implementing changes | 0 (no capacity) to 3 (yes, with team) |

- **6–9**: SEO is a real lever. Invest accordingly.
- **3–5**: SEO is supplementary. Don't prioritize over channels with stronger fit.
- **0–2**: SEO is the wrong focus right now. Revisit later.

This isn't a Google formula — it's a practical sanity check. Anyone selling you SEO services should be willing to walk through these with you honestly.

### What "investing in SEO" actually costs

Realistic ranges for businesses serious about SEO:

- **DIY** (founder or marketing manager does it themselves, 2–8 hours/week): $0–$200/month in tools. Works for small local businesses and early-stage startups.
- **Light professional support** (consultant on retainer, ~10 hours/month): $1,500–$5,000/month. Audits, strategy, occasional implementation.
- **Full agency or in-house** (content production + technical work): $5,000–$50,000+/month. Larger businesses with significant traffic dependence.

The range is huge because the work is so variable. The honest answer is: budget enough to actually move the needle. A $500/month "SEO service" that promises rankings is almost certainly selling commodity backlinks or boilerplate audits — it won't work.

### The 80/20 of DIY SEO

If you're a small business doing it yourself, the 80/20 is roughly:

1. **Google Business Profile** if you're local. Free, high-leverage, takes an hour to set up.
2. **Basic technical hygiene.** Working sitemap, no broken pages, fast loading, mobile-friendly. Many CMS platforms handle this for you.
3. **One genuinely good page per service or product** you offer. Don't make 50 thin pages. Make 5 strong ones.
4. **A real About page and contact information.** Authoritativeness signal.
5. **Real authorship and bylines** on any content.
6. **Internal linking** between related pages.

That's 80% of small-business SEO. Most "professional" services overcomplicate the rest.

### When NOT to hire an SEO

Don't hire if:

- You haven't validated the customer's search behavior (test with paid ads first).
- You can't act on recommendations (no content team, no dev resources).
- Your timeline is sub-90-days.
- Your business model doesn't depend on inbound at all.
- You're already ranking well for the queries that matter — leave it alone.

## An analogy: hiring a personal trainer

Hiring a personal trainer is a great idea if:
- You want to get fit but don't know how to structure workouts
- You'll show up consistently
- You have a reasonable timeline (months, not weeks)
- You'll follow the nutrition guidance

Hiring a personal trainer is a waste if:
- You want to lose 30 lbs by next week
- You won't actually show up
- You'll skip the nutrition part
- You're already in great shape and just need to maintain

SEO is the same. Great fit, real ROI for the right business in the right mode. Wasted money for the wrong fit. Be honest about which you are.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The local dentist who 5x'd in a year.**
A dentist hired an SEO consultant. The work: optimize their Google Business Profile, get reviews systematically, fix the 4 broken pages on their old website, add 6 well-targeted service pages with real photos and patient testimonials. Cost: $1,500/month for a year. Result: 5x more inbound inquiries from search by month 12.

**Scenario 2: The B2B SaaS that wasted $80K.**
A B2B SaaS startup with sales-led GTM hired a fancy SEO agency on a $5K/month retainer. They invested in content marketing aimed at top-of-funnel keywords. After 18 months: lots of blog traffic, almost zero pipeline. Their actual buyers don't search Google for solutions; they get them via referrals. They paused SEO and shifted to outbound sales. Pipeline went up immediately.

**Scenario 3: The founder who DIY'd it well.**
A solo founder running a niche SaaS product spent 4 hours a week writing one genuinely substantive article per month. After 18 months, she had 18 articles, an organic traffic baseline that drove 60% of her trial signups, and a brand reputation in her space. Total spend on SEO: ~$200 in tools. Total time: ~300 hours over 18 months. Compounding ROI for years afterward.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Hiring SEO without validating customer behavior.** Run a paid search test first to confirm people actually search for what you offer.
- **Expecting results in 30 days.** Not the right timeline; channel mismatch.
- **Buying $500/month "SEO services" that promise rankings.** Vapor. The honest minimum is significantly higher.
- **Hiring without internal capacity to act.** The audit is worthless without implementation.
- **Treating SEO as a one-time project.** It's an ongoing investment.
- **Ignoring SEO because "we're B2B."** Some B2B businesses do benefit (especially product-led / inbound-driven ones). Don't assume.

## Summary

- Three questions decide whether SEO is the right priority: customer search behavior, timeline fit, and commitment to implementation.
- SEO results take 4 months to a year. If you need revenue sooner, SEO is the wrong primary channel — though it can compound alongside paid acquisition.
- Realistic SEO budgets range from DIY/$0 to $50K+/month. $500/month "services" rarely deliver.
- For most small businesses, the 80/20 is: Google Business Profile, basic technical hygiene, one good page per service, real authorship, and internal linking.
- Don't hire SEOs you can't implement for. Don't hire them for businesses where customers don't search. Don't expect what they can't deliver.
