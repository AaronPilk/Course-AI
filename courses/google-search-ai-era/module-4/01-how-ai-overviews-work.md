# How AI Overviews and AI Mode actually generate answers

## The puzzle

You search a question. Above the regular results, Google shows an AI-generated summary that answers your question in a few paragraphs. It cites three sites in small links to the right. The summary is mostly correct and saves you a click.

Where did that summary come from? Is the AI making it up? Did it crawl the web in real time? Is your site doing anything that gets you *into* those citation slots — or kept out of them?

You need a working mental model of what's actually happening behind that summary. This lesson is it.

## The simple version

Google's AI features (AI Overviews, AI Mode) don't "know" things the way you might assume. They don't have answers baked into the model. Instead, they:

1. **Take your query**
2. **Search Google's regular index** (the same index that powers normal search)
3. **Retrieve a handful of relevant pages**
4. **Use a large language model to synthesize an answer grounded in those pages**
5. **Cite the pages they pulled from**

This pattern has a name: **Retrieval-Augmented Generation**, or **RAG**.

The same pages that rank well in normal Google search are the pages that feed AI Overviews. Optimizing for AI search isn't a different game — it's the same game with a few new properties.

## The technical version

### Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), explained

A naïve large language model (like an unmodified ChatGPT) generates answers from its training data. Two problems with that:

- **Training data has a cutoff.** A model trained in 2024 doesn't know about events after that.
- **The model can hallucinate.** It generates plausible-sounding statements that aren't true.

RAG solves both by inserting a retrieval step:

1. **User asks a question.**
2. **A search step retrieves a small number of relevant documents** (web pages, in Google's case) from a fresh, current index.
3. **The model generates the answer using those retrieved documents as the source material.**
4. **The model cites its sources.**

The retrieval step grounds the answer in real, current information. Hallucinations drop dramatically. And the user can verify by clicking the citations.

This is what Google means when they say AI features are "grounded" in Search. From Google's AI Optimization Guide:

> "Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG): A technique (also known as grounding) used to improve the quality, accuracy, and freshness of AI responses by relying on our core Search ranking systems to retrieve relevant, up-to-date web pages from our Search index."

That's the whole architecture in one sentence.

### Query fan-out — how AI search asks more than you asked

A subtler trick Google's AI features use: they don't just search for *your* exact query. They generate *several* related queries and search for each, then combine the results.

This is called **query fan-out**. Google's own description:

> "Query fan-out: A set of concurrent, related queries generated by the model to request more information and fetch additional relevant search results to address the user's query."

Example: a user types "how to fix a lawn that's full of weeds." A naïve search just looks for that exact string. AI Mode might also internally search for:

- "best herbicides for lawns"
- "remove weeds without chemicals"
- "how to prevent weeds in lawn"
- "lawn aeration for weed control"

Then it synthesizes an answer from all of those results.

What this means for your site:

- **Your page doesn't have to match the user's exact query word-for-word.** If your page is relevant to a *related* query the AI generates, it can still be cited.
- **Topical depth matters more.** A site that covers a topic from multiple angles is more likely to surface across the fan-out queries.
- **Exact-keyword optimization is even less effective than before.** AI features already understand synonyms and related concepts.

### What Google evaluates when picking sources

The AI features don't pick citation sources differently than regular search ranks pages. They pull from the same index using the same ranking signals. Pages that get cited in AI Overviews are pages Google was *already* going to rank highly in some configuration.

That means everything from Modules 1–3 applies directly:

- The page must be **crawlable** (Stage 1)
- The page must be **indexed** (Stage 2)
- The page must be **served as a relevant result** for the query or its fan-outs (Stage 3)
- The content has to satisfy the helpful, reliable, people-first guidelines (Module 2)
- The technical SEO has to be in order (Module 3)

There is no separate "AI search ranking algorithm." There's the same Google algorithm, with an AI layer on top that uses Google's existing ranking decisions as input.

### The clickability question

There's an obvious concern: if AI Overviews answer the user's question directly, does anyone still click through?

The honest answer is *sometimes*, depending on query type:

- **Informational queries with simple answers.** Often answered fully by the Overview. Click-through drops.
- **Informational queries needing nuance.** The Overview shows a summary; users still click to verify or get depth.
- **Transactional queries (buying, comparing).** Overview rarely satisfies. Users still click.
- **Local/intent-specific queries.** Overview is often less useful than a map result or direct site.

This shifts the SEO calculus:

- "What is X?" pages have lower click-through than they did three years ago.
- "Best X for Y" pages still drive clicks — comparison content survives the AI summary.
- Deep how-to / case-study content also survives — readers want the actual walkthrough.

We cover this in detail in the next two lessons.

### Image and video in AI features

Google's AI features can also pull images, videos, and structured data into their answers. From the AI Optimization Guide:

> "Many people appreciate finding images and videos as they search for things online. As with Google Search overall, our generative AI search features can bring in relevant images and video."

What this means: your image SEO (Module 3, lesson 4) feeds AI search too. A page with relevant, well-described images and videos can show up in AI features as the source for the visual element — sometimes even when the text on the page wouldn't have ranked.

This is one of the few cases where the rich-media optimization Google's been recommending for a decade pays off in a new way.

### How RAG affects "freshness"

Because the retrieval step always queries the current Search index, AI answers reflect *current* web content, not the model's training data.

That means:

- **Recently updated content can be cited.** A blog post you publish this week can appear in an AI Overview tomorrow.
- **Stale content quietly stops being cited.** When you stop updating, your pages can lose citation traffic before they lose normal rankings.
- **YMYL content needs to stay fresh.** AI features for health/finance queries especially favor recent, authoritative sources.

For most sites this means: regular update cadence on important pages matters more than it used to. Not for the date itself — for the substance.

## An analogy: the librarian who summarizes

You walk into a library and ask the librarian a question. The librarian doesn't have the answer memorized. Instead, they:

1. **Hear your question** and think about what books might cover it.
2. **Walk into the stacks** and pull three or four relevant books.
3. **Skim the relevant chapters** of each.
4. **Summarize what they read** back to you in their own words.
5. **Hand you the books in case you want to read them yourself.**

That's RAG. The librarian (Google's AI features) doesn't make up an answer — they synthesize one from real books on the shelf (the Search index).

If your book is in the library and on the topic the librarian's helping with, you might be one of the books they grab. If your book isn't in the library, or it's about a different topic, you're never going to be cited.

The way to be a "librarian's pick" isn't to write a book *for the librarian*. It's to write a *useful book on the right topic, organized clearly enough that the relevant chapter is easy to find and summarize.*

That's the whole strategy.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The depth-first content site.**
A founder publishes one in-depth, original article per month. After 18 months, she has 18 substantive articles in her niche. She's regularly cited in AI Overviews — not because she optimizes for them, but because her articles are exactly the kind of substantive, original, factually grounded material the AI's RAG pipeline wants to ground in.

**Scenario 2: The big brand's commodity-content loss.**
A major lifestyle publication has thousands of generic "how to X" articles. After AI Overviews launched, traffic on these articles dropped 30–60% for queries the Overviews answered well. They pivoted: kill the commodity articles, invest in deep originals. Six months later, the originals are getting cited and the brand's overall traffic is up. The lesson: commodity content is what AI summarizes; non-commodity is what it cites.

**Scenario 3: The technical docs site.**
A technical product's documentation site started appearing in AI Mode answers for technical queries about the product *and* about the underlying technology. They hadn't done anything specifically for AI search. Their docs were just well-structured, accurate, and the canonical reference. AI Overviews surfaced them because they were the best source the index had.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Believing AI features have a separate ranking algorithm.** They don't. Same index, same signals, with an AI synthesis layer on top.
- **Trying to write "for AI" with weird formatting tricks.** AI Overviews quote clear, normal content. Writing in pseudo-bullets, all caps, or chunked Q&A formats doesn't help.
- **Stuffing your page with every variation of every query.** The query fan-out makes this unnecessary — and the scaled-content-abuse policy makes it actively harmful.
- **Ignoring image and video SEO because "AI is text."** AI features pull rich media too.
- **Assuming AI features are stealing your clicks specifically.** They're shifting CTR by query type. Audit your traffic by query intent to see where you're affected.

## Summary

- AI Overviews and AI Mode use **Retrieval-Augmented Generation**: they search Google's regular index, retrieve relevant pages, and synthesize an answer from those pages with citations.
- They also use **query fan-out**: generating several related queries and combining the results. Topical depth matters more than exact-keyword matching.
- There is no separate AI ranking algorithm. Pages that rank well in normal Search are the pages that feed AI features.
- Click-through has shifted by query type. Commodity-informational queries lose clicks. Comparison and depth queries still drive clicks.
- The path to being cited in AI features is the path to ranking well in normal Search, applied with attention to clarity, originality, and freshness.
