# Who, How, Why — the three questions that decide if your content is real

## The puzzle

Two articles cover the same topic: "best practices for cold-emailing prospects."

Article A is anonymous. No byline. The site has no About page. The content is well-written but generic — five tips that could have been generated by anyone in any decade.

Article B has a clear byline: "Sarah Chen, 8 years at SDR-level positions at three SaaS companies." She links to her LinkedIn. The article opens with: "I sent 14,000 cold emails between 2018 and 2024. Here's what changed my reply rate from 1.8% to 11.2%." It includes screenshots of three of her actual sequences and a teardown of one that flopped.

Same topic. Same general claims. Which one would *you* trust? Which one would *Google's quality raters* trust?

That gut feeling — the one that makes Article B feel real and Article A feel hollow — is exactly what Google's Who / How / Why framework formalizes.

## The simple version

Google's own help docs propose three questions you can ask about any piece of content to evaluate whether it's the kind of content Google's systems want to surface:

1. **Who created the content?** Is it clear? Is there a byline, an author bio, a real human (or organization) behind the words?
2. **How was the content created?** Was it researched, written, and reviewed by people? Was AI involved? Is the methodology clear?
3. **Why was the content created?** Was it made to genuinely help a reader, or was it made to attract search traffic and hope visitors stick around?

These three questions cover the same territory as E-E-A-T, but from the *content author's* angle rather than the rater's. They're more useful for self-auditing because you can answer them honestly about your own work.

If you can answer all three clearly, your page is on solid footing. If any of them is murky, fix that — and you'll usually find rankings move with it.

## The technical version

### Who created the content?

From Google's docs:
> "Something that helps people intuitively understand the E-E-A-T of content is when it's clear who created it. That's the 'Who' to consider."

Practically, this means:

- **Is there a byline where one would naturally be expected?** A news article without a byline reads as anonymous and untrustworthy. A blog post about personal experience without a byline reads similarly. Reference content (like Wikipedia) doesn't always need a byline, but most editorial content does.
- **Does the byline link somewhere useful?** A byline like "By Sarah Chen" with no link is half a byline. A byline linking to an author page with bio, credentials, and other articles by the same author is a real byline.
- **Is the *organization* clear?** Who runs the site? Is there an About page? A way to contact someone real? Is the organization's interest in the topic legitimate?

Google explicitly recommends adding accurate authorship information. They don't recommend faking it — anonymous content from a credible organization can still rank, and named content from a credible expert generally ranks better than the same content unsigned.

### How was the content created?

This is the newest and most interesting part of the framework. Google added it as AI-generated content exploded:

> "It's helpful to readers to know how a piece of content was produced: this is the 'How' to consider including in your content."

The "How" answer is about *transparency about methodology*. Examples Google gives:

- **Product reviews**: How many products were tested? What were the test conditions? Did the reviewer actually use the products? Photos of the testing process build trust dramatically.
- **Recipes**: Did the author actually cook this? How many times? With what variations?
- **News reporting**: What sources were consulted? Were they on the record? Was the piece fact-checked?
- **AI-assisted content**: Was AI used to research, draft, or fact-check? What human review happened?

The "How" question is where Google's AI disclosure guidance lives. From the doc:

> "If automation is used to substantially generate content, here are some questions to ask yourself: Is the use of automation, including AI-generation, self-evident to visitors through disclosures or in other ways? Are you providing background about how automation or AI-generation was used to create content?"

This doesn't mean every AI-touched paragraph needs a "🤖" emoji next to it. But it does mean: if a reasonable reader would care how the content was produced, tell them.

### Why was the content created?

This is the most important of the three, and the one most people skip past. Google's docs:

> "'Why' is perhaps the most important question to answer about your content. Why is it being created in the first place?"

There are basically two answers:

- **To help people.** The content exists because there's a real audience for it; the author has something to share that would be useful even without search traffic.
- **To attract search traffic.** The content exists primarily as a vehicle for visibility on Google. If Google disappeared tomorrow, this content wouldn't exist.

Google's algorithm — and quality raters — are increasingly good at telling these apart. From the doc:

> "If you're primarily making content to attract search engine visits, that's not aligned with what our systems seek to reward."

This isn't a moral judgment about whether SEO is OK. SEO that *makes good content discoverable* is fine. SEO that *creates content for the sake of search* is what Google's "people-first" guidance is reacting against.

The Why question has a built-in lie detector: would you publish this content if Google didn't exist? If the answer is yes, your Why is real. If the answer is "uh, well, no" — your Why is search-engine-first.

### How this maps to E-E-A-T

E-E-A-T and Who/How/Why aren't separate frameworks — they're the same idea viewed from different angles.

| Who/How/Why | E-E-A-T |
|---|---|
| Who created the content? | Authoritativeness (who is this person/site?) |
| How was it created? | Experience + Expertise (what did they do, what do they know?) |
| Why was it created? | Trustworthiness (are the motives clean?) |

The Who/How/Why version is more useful for writers because it forces you to answer concretely. "Improve my expertise" is vague. "Make it obvious who wrote this and why they're credible to write it" is something you can actually do in an afternoon.

## A 10-minute Who/How/Why audit

Pick one of your most important content pages. Go through these in order:

**Step 1 — Read the page as if you've never seen the site before.**
Pretend you're a reader who arrived via a search result. Don't open the source. Just look at the rendered page.

**Step 2 — Answer Who, out loud:**
- Who wrote this? *(if there's no byline, you've already found a fix)*
- Why should I trust their take on this topic?
- What does the byline link to?

**Step 3 — Answer How, out loud:**
- How do I know the author actually knows / has done this?
- What evidence is on the page that would *prove* they did the work? (data, screenshots, specific examples, dates, named clients)
- If AI helped produce this, is that obvious?

**Step 4 — Answer Why, out loud:**
- Why does this article exist?
- If Google didn't exist, would this article still exist?
- Who exactly is the intended reader, and what specific problem are they trying to solve?

**Step 5 — Decide what's fixable in one sitting:**
- Most pages can fix Who in 30 minutes — add a byline, link to an author page, write or update the About page.
- How fixes are usually content additions — a paragraph on methodology, a couple of in-text screenshots, a citations section.
- Why fixes are bigger. If the answer is "this exists for search traffic," you usually need to either rewrite the page around a real audience or kill it.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The corporate blog without bylines.**
A B2B SaaS company has 200 blog posts attributed to "Acme Team." After a core update, half drop in rankings. They commission individual bylines for every post, written by the actual engineer or marketer who knows the topic. Author bios link to LinkedIn and an internal team page. Six months later, the strongest posts have not only recovered — they're ranking for queries they never targeted, because the author bios themselves became part of the topic relevance.

**Scenario 2: The AI-assisted research blog.**
A solo founder is publishing analysis posts about her industry. She uses AI heavily to research and draft. After a quiet patch in rankings, she adds a short "How this post was made" line at the bottom of each piece: "Draft assisted by AI; sources independently verified by [her name]; published [date]." Her rankings stabilize. Reader engagement (time on page) actually goes up — readers trust the post more once the methodology is transparent.

**Scenario 3: The "10 tips for..." factory.**
A content site has 2,000 listicles in the format "10 best tips for X." All anonymous. None of the tips are specific. After multiple core updates, traffic is two-thirds of its peak. They audit: Why does each article exist? Almost every answer is "to capture search traffic for 'best X tips' queries." They cull aggressively — kill 1,500 articles, rewrite the remaining 500 with specific authors and real, opinionated takes. Traffic recovers and surpasses peak.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Treating Who as optional.** Many sites assume their brand is enough. For most editorial content, individual human authorship is a stronger signal than brand-level attribution.
- **Hiding the How because it's "boring."** Readers love methodology when they're being asked to trust an opinion. A short "here's how I tested this" paragraph dramatically increases credibility.
- **Disclosing AI use defensively.** A short, factual disclosure ("draft assisted by AI, verified by [author]") is fine. Long disclaimers and apologies look guilty. Be matter-of-fact.
- **Answering Why with what you wish was true.** Be honest with yourself. If a page exists primarily to capture a keyword, admit it — then decide whether to make it genuinely useful or kill it.
- **Adding fake author pages.** Sites have started inventing fake authors with stock-photo headshots. Google's getting better at detecting this and it's one of the surer ways to lose trust permanently.

## Summary

- Three questions: **Who** created it, **How** was it created, **Why** was it created.
- Who/How/Why is the writer-facing version of E-E-A-T — more concrete and audit-friendly.
- Why is the most important question: if your content wouldn't exist without Google, it probably won't *rank* in Google either.
- Most content can be improved in an afternoon: add a real byline, write a short methodology paragraph, be honest about AI use.
- Cull pages where the honest Why is "search traffic" — or rewrite them around a real reader.
