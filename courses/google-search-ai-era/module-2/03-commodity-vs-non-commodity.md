# Commodity vs non-commodity content — the most important distinction nobody talks about

## The puzzle

Two SEO blogs both publish "7 tips for writing better cold emails." Same general advice. Same word count. Both well-formatted.

A year later, one has been quietly removed from Google's index. The other is referenced by an AI Overview when someone searches "cold email tips."

What's the difference?

The difference is the most important distinction in content strategy in 2026 — and Google buried it in plain English inside the AI Optimization Guide. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

## The simple version

Google now explicitly distinguishes two kinds of content:

- **Commodity content.** Generic advice on a topic that could have been written by anyone with access to a search engine. "7 tips for X." "Beginner's guide to Y." "What is Z?" The information itself is widely available; this version just packages it.

- **Non-commodity content.** Specific, expert, first-hand. Content that exists *because of who the author is*, not just because of the topic. Original research, lived experience, contrarian analysis, real case studies. The information *wouldn't exist* if this author hadn't created it.

Google's own example, from the AI Optimization Guide:

> "Commodity content (for example, something like '7 Tips for First-Time Homebuyers') is often based on common knowledge, which could originate from anyone, and typically adds little unique insight for readers. In contrast, non-commodity content (such as 'Why We Waived the Inspection & Saved Money: A Look Inside the Sewer Line') provides unique expert or experienced takes that go beyond common knowledge and the ordinary."

That parenthetical is the entire game.

In the AI search era, commodity content is increasingly worthless. AI Overviews can synthesize ten commodity articles into a single summary without sending traffic to any of them. Non-commodity content has a defensible moat — AI can summarize *what was said*, but it can only send users *back to the source* for the full experience.

## The technical version

### Why commodity content is dying

Three things are happening at the same time:

**1. Generative AI made commodity content cheap to produce.** A single prompt can generate "10 tips for first-time homebuyers" in 30 seconds. So the supply of commodity content has exploded.

**2. AI Overviews and AI Mode can synthesize commodity content without driving traffic.** When ten sites all say roughly the same thing, Google's AI features can summarize the consensus and present it directly. The user doesn't need to click any of the ten.

**3. Google's quality systems explicitly target commodity content.** The "people-first" framing, the scaled content abuse policy, the helpful content guidelines — all of it is, at its core, an attempt to discount generic, recycled content in favor of substantive original work.

The result: commodity content can still get crawled and indexed, but it increasingly fails to *serve* (Stage 3 from Module 1). It loses to:

- AI Overview summaries that quote *no one*
- Non-commodity content that has genuine insight
- High-authority brand content that has the marketing weight to win generic queries

### Why non-commodity content is winning

Non-commodity content has properties that *can't be summarized away*:

- **Specifics.** "I tested 14 of these. The cheap one performed best because of X." That sentence has information that doesn't exist anywhere else on the web. AI can quote it. AI can't replace it.
- **First-hand observation.** A photo of your sewer line, taken on the inspection day. A graph from your A/B test, with your traffic data on the axes. The information is *yours*.
- **Opinion grounded in experience.** "Most cold email advice tells you to personalize the first line. After 14,000 emails, I'm convinced personalization is overrated and tone matters more." That's a defensible claim because it's backed by experience.
- **Voice.** How you describe things. The metaphors you reach for. The asides. The structure. Commodity content tends to flatten these out. Non-commodity content keeps them.

When AI features summarize the web, they tend to *cite* the source for non-commodity content — because the specifics belong to that source. They tend to *replace* commodity content — because there's nothing specific to attribute.

### Where commodity content can still work

A few cases where commodity content isn't useless:

- **Brand sites with authority.** A bank publishing "how to read a mortgage statement" can rank for commodity content because the brand authority itself is the trust signal. A no-name blog publishing the same article cannot.
- **Specialty technical reference.** API docs, syntax references, configuration guides — content that's commodity by nature but you need *somewhere* to look it up. Google still serves these.
- **Internal helpfulness on a site that's mostly non-commodity.** If you have one foundational explainer page surrounded by twenty deep case studies, the explainer pulls weight from the case studies and can rank.

For most independent sites without huge brand authority, the playbook is clear: stop producing commodity content. Replace it with non-commodity.

### How to turn commodity into non-commodity

The rewrite is usually mechanical. Take any commodity piece and ask: what could *only this author / company / person* add to this?

Examples:

**Commodity:** "5 tips for better email subject lines."
**Non-commodity:** "I sent 14,000 cold emails. Here are the 5 subject-line patterns that doubled my reply rate — and the 3 that I expected to win but didn't."

The non-commodity version has:
- Specifics (14,000 emails, two replies multiplied)
- First-hand experience
- A contrarian angle (the ones that didn't work)
- Implied authority (you don't send 14,000 cold emails without learning something)

**Commodity:** "How to make sourdough bread."
**Non-commodity:** "I baked 200 loaves trying to understand why my crumb was tight. Turns out the temperature math everyone shares online is wrong — here's what's actually happening."

**Commodity:** "Beginner's guide to Kubernetes."
**Non-commodity:** "We migrated 47 services to Kubernetes over 18 months. Here are the four mistakes that cost us the most engineering time — including the one our entire industry seems to be making."

The pattern is consistent:

1. **Specific number** that signals scale or rigor.
2. **First-person framing** that establishes who did this.
3. **A contrarian or surprising hook** that makes the piece *about something* rather than *about a topic*.
4. **Promise of specific takeaways** that wouldn't exist anywhere else.

### The "would a real person bookmark this?" test

Google's helpful content self-assessment includes this question:

> "Is this the sort of page you'd want to bookmark, share with a friend, or recommend?"

For commodity content, the answer is almost never yes. Nobody bookmarks "10 tips for first-time homebuyers" — there are 50,000 of them on the web.

For non-commodity content, the answer is often yes. People bookmark and share content that has *specifics they want to come back to*.

If a smart friend wouldn't text you a link to this, the content is probably commodity. That's a surprisingly accurate gut check.

## An analogy: the photography market

In the 1990s, professional photographers made good money taking commodity photos: stock images of office workers, landscapes, food, generic happy families. Magazines and ad agencies bought them by the thousand.

Then digital cameras got cheap. Then phone cameras got good. Then iStockphoto and Shutterstock made stock photos a $1 commodity. Then AI made them effectively free.

A professional photographer who only took commodity stock photos in 2025 is out of business. The market for that work collapsed.

But professional photographers who specialize — wedding photographers who know your family, food photographers who do specific cuisines, journalists who get access nobody else has, fine art photographers with a distinctive voice — they're doing fine. Their work isn't a commodity. It has properties that can't be replicated by a phone or by AI.

Content is on the same trajectory. Commodity articles are stock photos. AI is the phone camera that made them cheap. Non-commodity content is what survives.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The B2B SaaS blog that cut 60% of its content.**
A SaaS company had 800 blog posts, mostly "what is X" and "best practices for Y" pieces. After multiple quality updates, organic traffic was down 40%. They audited every post against the commodity test. They cut 480 posts (the commodity ones), kept 320, and committed to publishing only non-commodity content going forward. Within 18 months, organic traffic recovered and the surviving posts ranked for queries they never targeted.

**Scenario 2: The recipe site that added 20 sentences per recipe.**
A small recipe blog was getting hammered by larger sites with template recipes. The author started adding a personal section at the top of every recipe: how she learned this dish, what she got wrong the first three times, what regional variation she prefers and why. The recipes themselves stayed the same. The headers became non-commodity. Rankings improved across the board.

**Scenario 3: The marketing agency that publishes deep case studies.**
A small marketing agency couldn't compete on volume with the big content marketing brands. Instead, they published one in-depth case study per month — real client work, real numbers, what worked, what didn't. After two years they had 24 case studies. Each one ranked for surprising adjacent queries. They became the default reference in their niche despite being a fraction of the size of their competitors.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Putting the personal touch only in the intro.** A two-paragraph "my story" before five paragraphs of generic advice is still commodity content. The non-commodity bits need to be threaded through the whole piece.
- **Faking specificity.** "I sent 14,000 emails" is great if it's true. If it's made up, eventually someone will notice — and you'll lose more credibility than you gained.
- **Defining "expert" too narrowly.** Non-commodity doesn't require a PhD. It requires *first-hand familiarity*. A new dad writing about sleep training based on his actual experience is non-commodity. A pediatrician writing about sleep training based on textbook patterns is more commodity than people realize.
- **Conflating long content with non-commodity content.** A 4,000-word article can be as commodity as a 600-word article. Length doesn't matter. Specifics matter.
- **Refusing to kill commodity content.** Keeping commodity content "just in case" usually hurts you. It dilutes your site's topical strength and competes for crawl budget. When in doubt, kill.

## Summary

- **Commodity content**: generic, recyclable, could have been written by anyone. Increasingly worthless in AI search.
- **Non-commodity content**: specific, first-hand, expert. Has properties AI can quote but not replace.
- Google explicitly contrasts these in its AI Optimization Guide. They are *not* using vague "quality" language — they're naming the distinction.
- The rewrite pattern: specific numbers + first-person framing + contrarian or surprising hook + promise of specific takeaways.
- For most independent sites, the playbook is: stop producing commodity content; rewrite or kill what you have; produce only non-commodity going forward.
