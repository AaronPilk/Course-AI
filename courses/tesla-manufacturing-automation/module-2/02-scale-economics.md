# Scale economics — why bigger factories beat lots of small ones

## The puzzle

Conventional wisdom says: build lots of regional plants close to demand. Reduces logistics. Provides political flexibility. Spreads risk.

Tesla bet the opposite: concentrate production in a small number of massive sites. Why does this work, and when does it break?

## The simple version

Three cost curves drive the case for scale:

1. **Fixed costs amortize**: plant management, software, R&D, training infrastructure spread across more units.
2. **Learning curves**: doubling cumulative output drives unit cost down by 10-30% (industry-dependent).
3. **Bargaining power**: massive volume = better supplier terms.

Plus: shared infrastructure across stages (cells next to packs next to vehicles) eliminates handoff costs.

When does it break? When demand uncertainty, geographic constraints, or political risk dominate scale gains.

## The technical version

### Fixed costs amortize

Every factory has fixed costs regardless of output:

- Plant management and admin overhead.
- IT infrastructure and software licenses.
- Engineering staff doing improvement work.
- Quality and compliance functions.
- Real estate and utilities baseline.
- Maintenance and tooling depreciation.

These costs are roughly the same whether you produce 100K vehicles or 500K per plant. So the fixed cost per vehicle is 5× lower at 500K than at 100K.

Multiply across many factory-supporting functions: training programs, R&D facilities, supply chain teams. Each one's per-unit cost drops with scale.

### Learning curves

Wright's law / experience curves: cumulative output predicts unit cost. Industry-specific rates:

- Batteries: historically ~25-30% cost reduction per doubling of cumulative output.
- Solar panels: similar.
- Mature mechanical components: 10-15%.
- Software: extremely high learning rates initially.

For a new technology like EVs, learning rates are high. The first 100K vehicles produced are expensive; the millionth is much cheaper. Tesla's bet was getting to millions fast.

This isn't magic — it's compounded improvement from every shift, every engineer, every iteration. But it's REAL, and it's why being early to scale matters disproportionately.

### Bargaining power

A factory making 1 million vehicles/year buys more steel, more glass, more electronics than one making 100K. Suppliers offer better terms:

- Lower per-unit cost.
- Better payment terms.
- Capacity commitments.
- Priority during shortages.

For commodity inputs especially, scale = bargaining power = lower cost.

### Shared infrastructure across stages

In a Gigafactory, cells → modules → packs → vehicles flow through one site. Each handoff that would have been a logistical event in a traditional supply chain becomes an internal transfer:

- No shipping.
- No inventory in transit.
- No FX risk between suppliers in different countries.
- No customs / tariffs.
- No coordination overhead with external partners.

These handoff costs are real and big. Eliminating them adds points to margin.

### The case for many small plants

Why do other manufacturers build many regional plants? Real reasons:

- **Tariffs and trade rules**: local production avoids import duties.
- **Political pressure**: governments want local manufacturing.
- **Logistics**: heavy or large products are expensive to ship far.
- **Demand variability**: smaller plants can be brought up/down more easily.
- **Labor cost differences**: arbitraging across geographies.

For some industries (food, beverages, basic consumer goods), these factors dominate scale economics. Many small plants is correct.

For EVs in the 2020s, scale economics win — for now. As markets mature and capacity catches up, balance might shift.

### Tesla's specific scale plays

The Gigafactory model is one scale play. Tesla also achieves scale through:

- **Common platform across models**: Model 3 and Model Y share ~75% parts; manufacturing infrastructure largely shared.
- **Limited model count**: 4-5 vehicle types instead of legacy auto's 20-40. Each gets enormous scale.
- **Software shared across all vehicles**: same OS, same firmware patterns, same data infrastructure.
- **Charging network as platform**: same Superchargers serve all Tesla owners, with growing third-party access.

Each layer compounds the others. Total scale advantage > sum of parts.

### Risks of concentration

Real downsides:

- **Single-point-of-failure risk**: a fire / earthquake / labor strike at a Gigafactory could shut down significant capacity. Tesla mitigates with geographic diversification but concentration risk remains.
- **Political risk**: depending on one country's regulatory environment for a large share of production.
- **Demand risk**: when demand softens, large fixed costs hurt more than smaller flexible plants.
- **Logistics risk**: serving customers far from production sites is expensive.

These risks are real and have hit Tesla. Concentrated production at Shanghai during COVID lockdowns illustrated the supply risk. Berlin during European demand softness illustrated the demand risk.

### The competitor response

Legacy automakers are torn:

- **Recognize the scale economics** of Gigafactory-style production.
- **Have existing plant networks** representing trillions in sunk capital.
- **Face political pressure** to keep traditional plants running.
- **Are partnering with cell makers** to scale battery production faster.

Result: a mixed strategy. Some legacy plants converted to EV production; some new Gigafactory-style plants built; cell partnerships rather than pure in-house. Whether this hybrid catches Tesla's structural advantages is open.

### When demand catches up

Tesla's scale economics work because demand for EVs has been growing. Build Gigafactory, fill it, build the next. When demand catches supply (as it might in 2027-2028 in some markets), the math changes:

- Underutilized capacity hurts unit cost.
- Plant flexibility becomes more valuable than raw scale.
- The premium for being first-to-scale erodes.

This is the maturity transition every growing industry hits. Tesla's response will likely be: produce more models from existing plants, expand into commercial vehicles (trucks, etc.), and lean further on energy storage business which uses similar infrastructure.

### Lessons for other industries

For other capital-intensive, growing markets:

- **Concentrate production** when scale curves are steep.
- **Diversify geographies** as markets mature to manage risk.
- **Common platform** strategies multiply scale benefits.
- **Shared infrastructure across stages** compounds.

Doesn't fit every industry, but where it does, the gains are large.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The Model Y scale dividend.**
Model Y shares ~75% parts with Model 3. Manufacturing lines were largely shared. The Model Y achieved Tesla's lowest cost per vehicle at the time of launch despite being a new model — because it could ride Model 3's compounded learning curve.

**Scenario 2: The Shanghai COVID lesson.**
When Shanghai went into lockdown, Tesla's Shanghai Gigafactory was offline. Significant portion of Tesla's quarterly output was affected. The concentration that gave Tesla scale also made them vulnerable to a single political/health event. Tesla added geographic diversification subsequently.

**Scenario 3: The legacy automaker dilemma.**
Ford has trucks built at large plants but EV production was initially scattered. Their strategy shifted to consolidate EV-specific production (the BlueOval City complex). Recognized scale economics but late to the structural commit.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Building lots of small plants** when scale curves are steep.
- **Underestimating concentration risk** — diversify geographically.
- **Ignoring shared infrastructure benefits** — across stages, models, software.
- **Assuming scale wins forever** — maturity transitions change the math.

## Summary

- **Fixed costs amortize** with output — bigger plants spread overhead.
- **Learning curves** compound — being early to scale matters disproportionately.
- **Bargaining power** scales with volume.
- **Shared infrastructure across stages** eliminates handoff costs.
- **Risks of concentration**: single-point failures, political exposure, demand variability.
- **When to diversify**: market maturity, geographic logistics, political risk.
- Tesla's scale plays: Gigafactories, common platforms, limited model count, shared software.
