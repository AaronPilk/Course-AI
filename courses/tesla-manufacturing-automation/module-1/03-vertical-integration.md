# Vertical integration — what Tesla owns vs. supplies

## The puzzle

For 40 years, the automotive industry moved toward outsourcing. OEMs (Toyota, Ford, GM, VW) shed in-house production of components and bought from specialist suppliers. By the 2010s, OEMs sourced 60-80% of vehicle content from a tiered supplier network.

Tesla bucked the trend. Tesla owns the cell manufacturing partnerships, the modules, the packs, the motors, the inverters, the body castings, the software stack, the charging network. They source more in-house than legacy auto.

Why? And when does it work?

## The simple version

Vertical integration is the choice to **make** something in-house instead of **buy** it from a supplier. Tesla owns what controls quality, cost, and cycle time. They source commodities and things they have no special advantage on.

This increases capital cost and operational complexity. The bet: control over critical components creates faster iteration, lower long-run costs, and reduced supply risk.

## The technical version

### The traditional outsourcing model

Legacy auto outsources because:

- **Capital efficiency**: suppliers absorb the capex of specialized factories.
- **Specialization**: suppliers focus on one component; achieve scale and learning curves.
- **Risk diversification**: failures isolated to one supplier.
- **Flexibility**: change suppliers if better options emerge.

Result: a Tier-1 supplier network specializing in specific systems (Bosch, Continental, Magna, ZF, etc.). OEMs assemble + integrate + handle some final processes.

### Why Tesla goes vertical

For early Tesla, vertical integration was forced — no supplier ecosystem existed for EVs at the scale needed. They had to build their own:

- Battery packs at automotive volume (Panasonic partnership for cells; Tesla owned modules/packs).
- Electric motors with their power density / cost targets.
- Power electronics (inverters, chargers).
- Battery management software.
- The Supercharger network (no third party existed).

Once these were running, vertical integration became a strategic choice. The pattern:

- **Own what determines product quality and cost** (batteries, motors, electronics, software).
- **Own what determines product cycle time** (body manufacturing where gigacasting accelerates iteration).
- **Buy commodities** (raw materials with some upstream moves; basic mechanical parts; tires).
- **Buy items where suppliers have insurmountable advantages** (some semiconductors, some chemistry).

### What Tesla owns

- **Battery packs and modules** (cells from partners; pack design / assembly in-house).
- **Electric motors** (full design and manufacturing).
- **Power inverters** (Tesla designed silicon carbide inverter — industry-leading at the time).
- **Software stack** (firmware, OS, infotainment, autopilot — all in-house).
- **Charging network** (Supercharger and Destination — Tesla operates).
- **Body castings** (gigacasting — Tesla designed and operates the casting machines).
- **Some chassis and structural components**.
- **AI compute infrastructure** (Dojo training cluster).

### What Tesla buys

- **Raw materials**: lithium, nickel, cobalt, copper, rare earths (with vertical moves upstream into mining partnerships).
- **Most semiconductors**: chips, processors, sensors (designed by Tesla in some cases, fabbed elsewhere).
- **Tires, glass, basic interior materials**.
- **Some sensors**: cameras, ultrasonics.
- **Cell chemistry** in some cases (from partners with proprietary chemistry).

### The capital cost

Vertical integration is expensive. Tesla's capex spending consistently runs higher than legacy auto on a percentage basis. Multiple gigafactories, vertical campuses, in-house manufacturing of components that suppliers could provide cheaper short-term.

The payoff: long-run unit cost reductions, faster iteration (don't wait for suppliers to redesign), reduced supplier-failure risk, learning curve compounding.

This is only feasible because Tesla had access to capital markets at scale — through software-style revenue valuations, follow-on equity raises, and high-margin product mix. A traditional carmaker with auto-industry valuations couldn't fund equivalent vertical integration.

### When vertical integration wins

It tends to win when:

- **Product cycle time matters more than per-unit cost optimization**.
- **You can iterate the component faster than suppliers can**.
- **Supplier failure or capacity constraints create real risk**.
- **You have proprietary process or design IP worth protecting**.
- **Capital is available at scale**.

For Tesla's batteries, the supplier ecosystem in 2008 simply couldn't deliver what they needed. Vertical was forced and right.

For a small startup making electric scooters in 2026, the supplier ecosystem is now mature — vertical integration would burn capital with no strategic gain.

### When vertical integration loses

It loses when:

- **The component is a commodity** with strong supplier competition.
- **Suppliers have learning curve advantages** you can't catch.
- **Capital is constrained** and core product needs that capital instead.
- **Demand is uncertain** — fixed costs of in-house production hurt during downturns.
- **The component changes slowly** — supplier optimization beats yours.

Most companies should NOT vertically integrate by default. The case has to be specific and economic.

### Risks of Tesla's integration

Real risks:

- **Concentration**: one gigacasting machine breaks → entire line stops. No supplier redundancy.
- **Capital intensity**: high recession risk if demand softens during heavy capex cycles.
- **Talent**: harder to hire skilled manufacturing engineers than to outsource the problem.
- **Innovation tradeoffs**: in-house teams might not adopt external innovations as quickly.

Tesla has hit these risks. Production hell during Model 3 launch (covered in Module 4) was partly a function of trying to vertically integrate too much too fast. They've recovered, but the risks are recurring.

### The upstream move

Tesla has been moving further upstream:

- **Lithium refining** partnerships and direct investment.
- **Nickel sourcing** from specific mines with quality commitments.
- **Battery cell chemistry** development (4680 cells with proprietary geometry).
- **Recycling** of end-of-life batteries.

This is unusual depth even for vertical integration. The bet: control of upstream materials becomes the competitive moat as EV adoption scales and material costs dominate vehicle cost.

### Lessons for other industries

Vertical integration is having a moment beyond auto:

- **SpaceX**: builds engines, vehicles, software, even some custom semiconductors.
- **Apple**: chips, displays, batteries, manufacturing partnerships.
- **Amazon**: AWS, logistics, devices, content.

Pattern: companies with capital, deep technical talent, and long-term horizons taking back control of pieces the industry had outsourced. Not universally correct; deliberate per case.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The battery breakthrough.**
Tesla's in-house battery work (with Panasonic and others) drove down cell cost faster than suppliers would have. Suppliers optimize for many customers; Tesla optimizes for Tesla. Result: kWh costs dropped faster than industry projections predicted. Vertical integration delivered.

**Scenario 2: The gigacasting bottleneck.**
A single Idra Group casting machine produces the rear underbody for Model Y. If that machine breaks, an entire shift loses Model Y production. Suppliers usually have multiple capacity sources. Tesla's vertical concentration is the cost of speed; production continuity is the risk.

**Scenario 3: The 'why don't they outsource this' moment.**
Tesla makes their own seats. Most automakers buy from suppliers. Tesla's seat manufacturing went through painful early years (production issues, quality complaints). They kept at it for control reasons. By 2026, Tesla seats are competitive but the road to get there was rough — a case where outsourcing might have been right.

## Common mistakes to avoid (in considering vertical integration)

- **"Vertical because Tesla does it"** — Tesla had specific reasons; copy the reasoning, not the playbook.
- **Underestimating capital needs** — vertical is capex-heavy and persistent.
- **Underestimating operational complexity** — running a factory is harder than buying parts.
- **Ignoring talent constraints** — manufacturing engineering is scarce and expensive.
- **Skipping the math** — vertical integration should pay back in concrete dollars or strategic moats.

## Summary

- **Vertical integration**: make in-house vs. buy from suppliers.
- Tesla owns **batteries, motors, electronics, software, charging, body castings**.
- Tesla buys **raw materials (some), most semis, basic mechanical parts**.
- **Capital-intensive**: only viable with deep funding access.
- **Wins when** cycle time + iteration speed + supply risk dominate.
- **Loses when** components are commodities, demand is uncertain, capital is constrained.
- **Risk**: concentration when components have single points of failure.
