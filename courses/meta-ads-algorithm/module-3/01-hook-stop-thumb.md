# The hook — stopping the thumb

## Where attention dies

Average Meta ad watch time is under 4 seconds. The thumb is moving. Whether the user stops depends almost entirely on the first frame and the first sound. Everything else in the ad — the value proposition, the social proof, the offer, the CTA — only happens if the hook earns it.

This is the highest-leverage lever in your account. You can have a great offer, perfect targeting, and clean tracking, but if the hook doesn't stop scrolling, none of it matters. Conversely, a strong hook can carry an average offer.

## What a hook is

A hook is the first 1-3 seconds of a video ad (or the headline + main visual on a static). Its only job: make the user stop scrolling long enough to absorb the next 5 seconds.

Hooks work via three mechanisms, often combined:

**Pattern interrupt.** Something visually unexpected. A weird angle. Sudden motion in an otherwise still scene. A face looking directly at camera. Bright color against a duller feed. The thumb pauses to figure out what they're seeing.

**Curiosity gap.** A spoken or written claim that creates an open loop the brain wants closed. "I tried this for 30 days and the result surprised me." "Most people are wearing the wrong size — here's how to tell." The user holds attention to find out what closes the loop.

**Self-identification.** The hook signals "this is for you" to your target buyer and "not for you" to everyone else. "If you train under 10 hours a week, this is the protein you actually need." A specific identifier slices the audience and pulls in the right slice.

## The hook formats that work

A non-exhaustive list of formats with proven traction:

**The talking head.** Person looks directly into camera and makes a specific statement in the first sentence. Works when the speaker has credibility (founder, customer, expert) or charisma.

**The contrast cut.** Before/after, with/without, problem/solution — visually contrasted in the first second. Skin care, fitness, home cleaning all use this.

**The unboxing or first use.** "Just got this in the mail." Visual product reveal in the first frame. Works for physical products with visual appeal.

**The objection or skepticism opener.** "I didn't think this would work either." Disarms resistance immediately.

**The data hook.** "9 out of 10 [audience] are doing [X] wrong." Specific number creates curiosity and authority.

**The trend or news angle.** "Everyone's switching to [category] for this reason." Works with care — needs to feel real, not opportunistic.

**The negative space.** Quiet, slow, almost-still opening that makes the feed suddenly feel calm. Counter-pattern interrupt. Less common but striking when it works.

## Hook anti-patterns

Common openers that lose:

- Brand logo card with music swell — feels like a TV ad, scroll continues.
- Wide establishing shots — viewer doesn't know what they're looking at fast enough.
- "Hi everyone, today I want to talk to you about..." — preamble before payload.
- Generic stock footage — pattern-match to "ad," thumb keeps moving.
- Overlay text that says "AD" or "SPONSORED" stylistically — telegraphs ad nature.

The mistake is treating the hook as throat-clearing before the real content. There's no real content if the hook fails.

## Sound design matters

About 80% of mobile video plays with sound off, but the 20% with sound on is your conversion-likely audience — people who stopped enough to enable audio. Design for both:

**Sound-off:** caption the first sentence on screen, lead with strong visual.
**Sound-on:** start with a clear human voice (no music wash), the first sentence is the hook.

Don't open with music alone — it doesn't carry information.

## The hook isn't the headline

For static ads, the headline below the image is rarely the hook. The image is the hook. The headline is a supporting frame for users who already paused.

Test image-led versus copy-led variants. In most accounts, the image carries more weight than headline copy for stop rate. Copy matters for conversion, less for stop.

## How to measure hook performance

Two key metrics inside Ads Manager:

**Hook rate / 3-second video plays / impressions.** Percentage of impressions that became 3-second plays. Industry baseline is 25-35%; strong is 40-50%. Below 20% means the hook is failing.

**Thumb-stop rate (custom metric you build):** 3s video plays / unique reach. Similar lens, deduplicated by user.

Compare across creatives in the same ad set with similar reach. The hook-rate difference between top creative and bottom creative is usually 2-3x.

## Hook-test workflow

A clean hook test:

1. Same body of video (5-30 seconds after the hook) across variants.
2. Swap the first 2-3 seconds: different opening line, different opening visual, different first action.
3. Run 5-7 variants in one ad set, equal exposure.
4. After 7-10 days, look at hook rate (3s plays / impressions).
5. Winner becomes the new control. Iterate.

Don't test full creatives against full creatives when isolating hooks — too many variables. Test only the first 2-3 seconds.

## Hook iteration is the highest-ROI work

Most accounts plateau because the hook library is stale. Three to five hooks rotated for a year. The fix is treating hook generation as a continuous workflow:

- Weekly: 3-5 new hook concepts per offer.
- Monthly: 10-15 hook variants tested.
- Quarterly: hook library refresh — kill bottom performers, scale top variants.

Even with the same body content, fresh hooks compound performance.

## Mistakes to avoid

- **Reusing winning hooks too long.** Frequency kills them; refresh quarterly even when they're working.
- **Optimizing the wrong metric.** A hook that boosts hook rate but drops conversion rate may have hooked the wrong users. Watch both.
- **Hooking with curiosity that the body doesn't pay off.** Bait creates clicks but no sales; trust erodes.
- **Sound-on hooks for sound-off audiences.** Always design dual-mode.
- **Forgetting captions.** 80% of mobile video is sound-off; uncaptioned voiceover is wasted.

## Summary

- Hook = first 1-3 seconds; almost the entire ad's job.
- Mechanisms: pattern interrupt, curiosity gap, self-identification.
- Hook rate (3s plays / impressions): 25-35% baseline, 40-50% strong.
- Test hooks in isolation, not full creatives.
- Iterate continuously — stale hooks kill plateaus.
