# J-cuts, L-cuts, and the rhythm of dialogue

## Why dialogue needs special handling

Cuts where video + audio change together feel abrupt:
- Speaker A finishes; cut; Speaker B starts; cut; Speaker A again. Mechanical.
- Real conversation overlaps: you hear someone start talking before you see them.

J-cuts and L-cuts add naturalism. Audio leads or trails the picture; conversations breathe.

## J-cut

Audio FROM the next clip starts before the picture cuts to it.

Visual: in timeline, the audio edit point is to the LEFT of the video edit point (looks like a "J").

Example: Wide shot of room; we hear Speaker B's voice off-screen (J-cut); then cut to Speaker B speaking on-camera.

Use for: introducing speakers; revealing source of voice; layering scenes.

## L-cut

Audio FROM the current clip continues after the picture cuts to the next.

Visual: audio edit point to the RIGHT of video edit point (looks like an "L").

Example: Speaker A talking; cut to listening reaction shot (Speaker B); but Speaker A's audio continues.

Use for: showing reaction without breaking conversation; emotional weight on the listener's face; smoothing transitions.

## Setting up J/L cuts

**Method A — Manual:**
1. Cut V (Cmd+K with video only targeted).
2. Cut A separately (Cmd+K with audio only targeted) at a different point.
3. Result: video edit at one point, audio edit at another.

**Method B — Unlink + Trim:**
1. Select clip; Cmd+L to unlink V from A.
2. Use Ripple or Selection to trim V or A independently.
3. Re-link if desired.

**Method C — Auto-extend:**
1. Select edit point with Ripple.
2. Hold Option (Mac) / Alt (Win) → trim only V or only A by selecting that specific edge.

Method B (unlink) is the workflow most pros use for many J/L cuts in a sequence.

## Dialogue editing rhythm

A good dialogue cut:
- **Pause beats.** Cut on a slight pause; the speaker takes a breath; less jarring.
- **Cut to listener.** L-cut to reaction; lets the audience read emotion.
- **Cut on action.** If speaker gestures, cut on the gesture; motion masks the cut.

Bad dialogue cut:
- **Mid-syllable.** Words chopped; ugly.
- **No reaction.** Cuts feel mechanical.
- **Same height/shot type repeatedly.** Visual rhythm boring.

## The 6-second principle

For talking-head content, viewers want a visual change every 6-8 seconds (approximate).

If a speaker is on-camera for 30 seconds: B-roll over portions; cut to listener; vary angle.

L-cuts to reaction or B-roll covers visual monotony while keeping the audio flowing.

## Pacing — fast vs. slow

**Fast cuts** (1-2 seconds per shot):
- Action sequences.
- Music videos.
- Energy / momentum.

**Slow cuts** (5-15+ seconds):
- Documentary interviews.
- Cinematic moments.
- Emotional weight.

Vary within a piece: fast intro, slower exploration, fast resolution. Rhythm = engagement.

## Eyelines

In dialogue: characters should look "toward" each other across the cut.

If A looks screen-right, cut to B looking screen-left. Reverse violates the 180-degree rule and disorients.

For interviews: looking off-camera at different sides depending on if they're the "primary" or "responder."

## Cutting on motion

Cuts feel smoother when motion continues across:
- Speaker turns head → cut → other speaker mid-motion.
- Hand moves across frame → cut → continued movement.
- Camera pans → cut → starts panning.

Motion masks the cut. Without motion, cuts are static and obvious.

## Audio scrubbing

Hold Shift + drag the playhead → scrub with audio enabled. Helps find the exact frame of a syllable.

Or: J/K/L slow + listen. K (pause) + L (slow forward) reveals fine audio detail.

For frame-precise dialogue cuts: audio scrubbing is essential.

## Audio waveforms in timeline

Enable: timeline right-click → Display Audio Waveform (or wrench icon → Show Audio Waveform).

Visible peaks show where words happen. Cut between waves = silent gap → clean cut.

For dialogue editing: waveforms visible is non-negotiable.

## Removing breaths

A common edit: remove the gap between sentences where the speaker breathes.

1. Find the gap visually (waveform shows quiet section).
2. Set in/out around the breath.
3. Extract (apostrophe).
4. Result: tighter pace; speaker sounds more confident.

For heavy-edit interviews (YouTubers): this is 80% of the edit. Remove the dead air; you've made the content snap.

## Frequency control

Don't over-edit:
- Removing every pause = unnatural.
- 5 cuts in a sentence = jumpy.
- A breath here-and-there = humanity.

The art is judging when a pause is dramatic vs. dead.

## Reaction shots

For dialogue:
- **A speaks.** Show A.
- **A continues speaking; we cut to B.** B's reaction visible during A's audio (L-cut).
- **A finishes; B starts.** Audio from B begins under A's last word (J-cut into B's audio); video cuts after.

This is the documentary / film standard pattern. Creates flow.

## Transitions vs. cuts

For most edits: hard cut (instant change). Default.

For specific cases: short cross dissolve (0.5-1s) — passage of time, emotional shift. Long dissolves (2s+) feel old-fashioned.

Other transitions (wipes, slides, fancy stuff) — rare; usually wrong; trust the cut.

Sound bridges (audio carries across a cut) feel cleaner than visual transitions.

## Match cuts

Cut between two shots with similar composition/motion:
- Person walking left → cut → another person walking left.
- Hand reaches into frame → cut → similar hand reaching.

Match cuts hide the cut; the eye flows continuously.

## Smash cuts

Opposite of match cut: abrupt cut to wildly different content. Often comedic or shocking.

Both styles work; pick by intent.

## Mistakes to avoid

- **Cutting mid-syllable.** Ugly; redo.
- **All audio + video together.** Robotic.
- **Same shot length throughout.** Boring rhythm.
- **No B-roll over talking heads.** Visual monotony.
- **Too many cuts on tiny things.** Jittery; viewer exhausted.

## Summary

- J-cut: audio of next clip starts before picture cuts to it. Introduces; lays foundation.
- L-cut: audio of current clip continues after picture cuts. Reaction shots; flow.
- Set up via unlink + separate trim, or cut V and A at different points.
- Audio waveforms visible; audio scrub for frame precision.
- Cut on motion; cut on pauses; cut to reactions; cut on breath gaps.
- Vary pace; match cuts smooth; smash cuts disrupt.
