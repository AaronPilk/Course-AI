# Stainless steel, Raptors, and manufacturing approach

## The puzzle

Starship's manufacturing approach looks more like shipbuilding than aerospace. Stainless steel rolled into cylinders, welded together, painted (or not), rolled out to test stands. Raptor engines made by the dozens per month, each one cheaper than the last. The whole operation runs at a pace closer to automotive than rocket production.

How is this possible, and what does it tell us about how rockets get built now?

## The simple version

Three pillars of Starship manufacturing:

1. **Stainless steel structures** rolled and welded in shipyard-style facilities.
2. **Raptor engines** built at increasing rate with simplified designs each generation.
3. **Iterative test campaigns** with multiple test articles, each one teaching the next.

The factory at Starbase (Boca Chica) builds at a rate that traditional aerospace would consider impossible. The manufacturing approach is part of the strategic bet — Starship can't deliver on its economic targets without high production cadence.

## The technical version

### Stainless steel in detail

Starship uses primarily 301-stainless steel (and similar austenitic stainless grades). Properties:

- **Strength at cryogenic temperatures**: gets stronger as it gets cold, perfect for propellant tanks.
- **Heat resistance**: handles 1500C+ during reentry without melting (though tiles still help peak heating).
- **Weldability**: rolled, formed, welded in shipyard facilities.
- **Cost**: dramatically cheaper than aerospace aluminum-lithium or composites.

The vehicle is built from rolled steel sheets ~4-5mm thick, formed into cylindrical sections, welded. Each Starship has ~50 cylindrical "ring" sections stacked and joined to form the body.

Stainless steel is denser than aluminum. Structurally, this means thicker walls relative to mass. SpaceX accepts this penalty for the heat and manufacturing advantages.

### Starship manufacturing flow

The Starbase production flow:

1. **Steel rolls arrive** from suppliers (US-sourced where possible).
2. **Rolled into rings** in production tents.
3. **Welded into cylindrical sections** in stacking facilities.
4. **Sections stacked** to form the full vehicle.
5. **Engines integrated** at the bottom.
6. **Tile installation** on the windward side.
7. **Final assembly**: avionics, plumbing, thermal protection.
8. **Roll out** to test stand or launch pad.

The facility doesn't look like traditional aerospace. More like an industrial yard — tents, gantries, rolling-stock-style equipment. SpaceX has invested heavily in this footprint at Boca Chica.

### Raptor manufacturing

Raptor engines are built primarily at SpaceX's McGregor (Texas) and Hawthorne (California) facilities. Raptor 2's manufacturing simplifications:

- **Fewer parts** than Raptor 1 (~40% reduction in component count for some subsystems).
- **Integrated welds** replacing fasteners.
- **Simplified plumbing** routed for easier production.
- **Common parts** across sea-level and vacuum variants where possible.

Raptor 3 reportedly continues these optimizations: more integration, fewer fasteners, simpler tooling. Target: build hundreds per year at cost approaching $250K or lower per engine.

Compare to traditional rocket engines:

- **F-1 (Saturn V)**: each engine cost millions in 1960s dollars, hand-built.
- **SSME**: ~$40M each, extensive between-flight refurbishment.
- **RS-25 (SLS)**: ~$146M each (refurbished from Shuttle inventory).

Raptor's <$250K target represents a 100-1000x cost reduction per engine vs. legacy. The cost reduction is fundamental — it's why Starship economics can theoretically work.

### Test campaigns and iteration

Starship development uses iterative test campaigns:

- **Build multiple test vehicles** (S20, S24, S25, etc.) with progressive design changes.
- **Test each one** — static fire, hop test, atmospheric flight, orbital attempt.
- **Lose some test vehicles** to learn what failed.
- **Apply learnings** to the next test article.

This is dramatically faster than legacy aerospace, which often waits years between major iterations. SpaceX iterates in months. Some flights end in vehicle loss; SpaceX accepts this as the cost of fast learning.

This iterative approach has been controversial — critics argue SpaceX is reckless or "blowing up rockets for marketing." Defenders point to the actual hardware progress: catches working, reentry tiles improving, engine reliability climbing. The pattern is iterative engineering at speed.

### Cost reduction trajectory

SpaceX has publicly discussed the cost trajectory for Starship:

- **Early Starship test vehicles**: cost likely $50-100M each (specialized prototypes).
- **Operational Starships at scale**: target $10-20M each.
- **Raptor engines**: target <$250K each at scale.
- **Per-launch cost (operational)**: target <$10M, eventually <$2M.

The cost gap between targets and reality narrows with each generation. Whether the targets are achieved depends on operations, manufacturing scale, and reuse cycles.

### Manufacturing as engineering constraint

A recurring SpaceX theme: design for manufacturing, not just performance. Examples:

- **Stainless steel** chosen partly for shipyard-style manufacturability.
- **Raptor 2** redesigned for fewer parts.
- **Engine count** (33 + 6) chosen partly for manageable manufacturing scale per engine.
- **Booster catch** eliminates landing leg manufacturing.

This is unlike traditional aerospace where manufacturing comes after engineering. SpaceX deliberately constrains designs by what can be made at the required rate. The discipline produces different choices.

### Where things are made

SpaceX's manufacturing footprint:

- **Hawthorne, CA**: Falcon 9 / Heavy stages, Merlin engines, Dragon capsules. Primary HQ.
- **McGregor, TX**: engine testing, Merlin and Raptor production.
- **Boca Chica, TX (Starbase)**: Starship manufacturing, test launch site.
- **Vandenberg, CA**: launch operations.
- **Cape Canaveral, FL**: launch operations.

The "factory" is distributed across the US. Components flow between sites. Final assembly happens at the launch site for Starship; integration happens at the launch site for Falcon 9.

### Workforce and culture

SpaceX has ~20,000-25,000 employees (estimates vary). Compare to Boeing's ~140,000 or Lockheed Martin's ~100,000. SpaceX produces more launches with a smaller workforce.

The culture is famously intense: long hours, high expectations, rapid iteration, willingness to lose hardware for learning. This is not for everyone. SpaceX retention varies by team and time — propulsion engineers, for example, tend to stay because the work is compelling. Other roles have higher turnover.

For manufacturing specifically, SpaceX has built up significant production engineering talent. The Tesla-SpaceX overlap is real here — both companies emphasize production engineering as a discipline.

### Comparison with Tesla manufacturing

Many themes overlap between SpaceX and Tesla:

- Vertical integration (build everything in-house).
- Software-defined manufacturing (instrument and iterate).
- Aggressive manufacturing redesigns (Tesla's gigacasting; SpaceX's Raptor 2/3 simplifications).
- Iterative test campaigns.
- Production engineering as core discipline.

Differences:

- Tesla: high volume, repetitive product. Tens of thousands of identical vehicles.
- SpaceX: lower volume, more variation. Hundreds of rockets, more individual customization per launch.
- Tesla: consumer end-user. SpaceX: launch customers and own Starlink.

Both companies are case studies in modern manufacturing applied to historically slow industries.

### Risks of the manufacturing approach

Real risks:

- **Quality at speed**: rapid iteration can mask quality issues until they emerge in flight.
- **Talent dependency**: high-pace cultures can burn people out.
- **Capital intensity**: factories at Boca Chica and elsewhere are major capex.
- **Concentration**: most Starship production at one Texas site has natural disaster exposure (storms, etc.).
- **Regulatory**: high cadence requires range cooperation that can be politically fragile.

SpaceX has mitigated through redundancy (multiple sites), insurance, and political relationships. But the risks are real.

### Where this is going

Starship manufacturing is targeted to scale dramatically:

- 2024-2025: producing perhaps 6-12 Starships per year, similar Super Heavies.
- Target: 50-100 per year by late 2020s.
- Long-term vision: thousands per year (for Mars campaigns and large-scale orbital operations).

Whether these scale targets are achieved depends on demand (real customers, including Starlink-2, lunar, Mars) and capital. The factory keeps growing.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: The Raptor cost trajectory.**
Each Raptor generation has been cheaper than the last. Raptor 1: relatively expensive prototypes. Raptor 2: ~$1M each by 2022 estimates. Raptor 3: targeting <$250K each at scale. The reduction comes from simpler designs, more automation, and serial production. By the time Starship operates at scale, engines must be cheap enough that an entire vehicle's worth of engines (~39) costs less than $10M.

**Scenario 2: The stainless steel decision in 2019.**
Musk announced the carbon-composite-to-stainless-steel switch publicly. Many in aerospace were skeptical. Five years later, Starships are flying; the steel decision worked. The decision wasn't just about material — it was about manufacturing approach. Steel allows the shipyard model; composites require slow, specialized layup processes.

**Scenario 3: A Starship test flight ending in vehicle loss.**
Several early Starship and Super Heavy test flights ended in explosions or vehicle loss. SpaceX investigated each one (engine anomaly, propellant management issue, valve failure), applied learnings to the next vehicle, kept flying. The iterative cost of vehicle loss in early development is offset by the learning. This wouldn't work in legacy aerospace where each vehicle is expected to fly successfully and failure stops the program.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Underestimating manufacturing as a design driver** — it shapes everything.
- **Treating Raptor cost claims uncritically** — targets, not yet achieved.
- **Ignoring iteration speed** — SpaceX's tempo is part of the moat.
- **Comparing to one-shot legacy programs** — SpaceX learns across many cycles.
- **Forgetting the workforce intensity** — it has costs.

## Summary

- **Stainless steel** allows shipyard-style manufacturing, heat resistance, low cost.
- **Raptor** engines cost-engineered each generation (Raptor 3 targets <$250K).
- **Iterative testing** with multiple test vehicles teaches the program.
- **Manufacturing scale** is critical — target 50+ Starships/year by late 2020s.
- **Boca Chica** is the primary Starship facility; Hawthorne for Falcon and engines.
- **Workforce intensity** is real but produces results.
- **Risks**: quality at speed, capital, concentration, regulatory.
- **Manufacturing as design constraint** is a recurring SpaceX theme.
