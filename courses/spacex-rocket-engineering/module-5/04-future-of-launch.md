# The future of launch — competitors and what's next

## The puzzle

SpaceX has dominated launch for a decade. But the landscape is changing: Blue Origin's New Glenn is reaching operational capability, Rocket Lab's Neutron targets medium-lift, Chinese rockets are scaling, ULA's Vulcan competes for government missions. Plus the longer-term question of what happens when Starship operational and what Mars and lunar exploration actually look like in practice.

What's coming, and what should we expect by 2030 and beyond?

## The simple version

The 2026-2030 launch landscape likely:

1. **SpaceX dominant**: Falcon 9 / Heavy continues high cadence; Starship scales up.
2. **Blue Origin emerges**: New Glenn reaches operational capability; BE-4 engines deploy widely.
3. **Chinese rockets scale**: Long March family + new private Chinese rockets compete globally on price.
4. **ULA repositions**: Vulcan replaces Atlas V and Delta IV; targets government and select commercial.
5. **Rocket Lab grows**: Electron + Neutron serve dedicated launch market.
6. **New entrants**: Relativity (3D printed rockets), Stoke (full reuse), others.

Plus:

- **Mega-constellations** drive launch demand (Starlink, Amazon Kuiper, others).
- **NASA Artemis** progresses through lunar landings.
- **Mars activity** remains aspirational but pieces falling into place.
- **In-space economy** emerges with manufacturing, refueling, lunar resources.

## The technical version

### SpaceX's trajectory

Looking at 2025-2030:

- **Falcon 9 / Heavy**: continued routine operations; cadence stays high; gradual fleet refresh.
- **Starship**: scales from test launches to operational missions; multiple variants deploy.
- **Starlink**: continues constellation expansion; revenue grows; potential IPO eventually.
- **NASA Artemis HLS**: lunar landing demos; eventual crewed lunar missions.
- **Mars**: cargo missions late in this period; possibly first uncrewed surface activity.

SpaceX's relative position: maintains dominance unless major setback. New Glenn and others compete in some segments but don't displace SpaceX overall.

### Blue Origin and New Glenn

Blue Origin has spent ~20 years developing New Shepard (suborbital) and New Glenn (orbital). New Glenn first launch attempts in 2024-2025.

New Glenn characteristics:

- **Reusable first stage** (similar concept to Falcon 9 but different design).
- **BE-4 engines** (methane + LOX, oxygen-rich staged combustion) — also powering ULA Vulcan.
- **7-meter diameter payload fairing** (larger than Falcon 9's).
- **Heavy-lift capability** (~45 t LEO, similar to Falcon Heavy).

By 2030, New Glenn likely:

- Achieves operational status.
- Captures some commercial market share.
- Provides US-government redundancy alongside Falcon and Vulcan.
- Powers Blue Origin's lunar mission strategy.

Blue Origin has been slower than SpaceX. The pace may accelerate.

### Chinese launch ecosystem

China has multiple launchers:

- **Long March family**: large state-run rocket family.
- **Long March 5**: heavy-lift; serves lunar and other missions.
- **Long March 8**: increasing reusability.
- **Private Chinese rockets**: LandSpace, Galactic Energy, iSpace, others.
- **Long March 9**: super-heavy in development (Saturn V class).

Chinese rockets:

- Launch at lower prices in some segments.
- Serve Chinese domestic market and exports.
- Demonstrate technical capability comparable to SpaceX in some areas.
- Build their own constellations (China's Guowang competing with Starlink).

US customers (especially DoD) cannot use Chinese launches. But Chinese rockets dominate the non-Western launch market and increasingly compete in third-country markets.

By 2030: Chinese launch capability rivals SpaceX in volume; competes on price in commercial segments where allowed; supports Chinese deep-space programs (lunar, Mars).

### ULA Vulcan

United Launch Alliance (ULA, joint venture of Boeing and Lockheed):

- **Replaced** Atlas V (which used Russian RD-180 engines).
- **Vulcan-Centaur** uses Blue Origin's BE-4 engines.
- **First launch** 2024.
- **Target**: DoD/Intelligence missions, NASA missions, some commercial.

ULA strategy:

- Heritage relationships with government customers.
- More conservative engineering than SpaceX.
- Pricing higher than SpaceX but lower than legacy Atlas V.
- Eventually some smart reuse (engine recovery via parachute).

By 2030: ULA likely retains significant government market share; struggles in commercial against SpaceX prices.

### Rocket Lab and dedicated small launch

Rocket Lab:

- **Electron**: small launcher (300 kg LEO); 50+ launches by 2025.
- **Neutron**: medium-lift reusable launcher; first flights 2025-2026.
- **Acquired Space Systems** business; vertical integration into satellites.

Rocket Lab is the most commercially successful new launcher company besides SpaceX. By 2030: Neutron operational, dedicated launch capacity for medium payloads, possibly competing for some Falcon 9 missions.

### New entrants

Multiple companies pursuing reusable launch:

- **Relativity Space**: 3D printed rockets (Terran R targets reuse).
- **Stoke Space**: full reuse architecture targeted from day one.
- **ABL Space**: small launcher with US Air Force focus.
- **Many others**: Phantom Space, Astra (struggling), various Chinese private companies.

Most of these will fail or get acquired. A few may emerge as durable players.

### The mega-constellation driver

Starlink + Amazon Kuiper + China's Guowang + others = enormous launch demand:

- **Starlink**: 6,000+ satellites in 2025; tens of thousands planned.
- **Kuiper**: ~3,200 satellites planned by Amazon.
- **Guowang**: 13,000+ Chinese constellation.
- **OneWeb**: ~650 satellites already in orbit.
- **Many others**: smaller commercial and government constellations.

Each constellation requires hundreds to thousands of launches. This demand sustains the launch industry across multiple providers. Without mega-constellations, launch demand would be much lower.

### NASA Artemis progression

NASA's Artemis program targets human lunar landings:

- **Artemis 1** (2022): uncrewed lunar orbit, successful.
- **Artemis 2** (planned 2026): crewed lunar flyby.
- **Artemis 3** (planned 2026-2027, will likely slip): first crewed lunar landing via Starship HLS.
- **Artemis 4+**: lunar surface base development.

SpaceX's Starship is critical path for Artemis 3+. Schedule risk is high. NASA depends on Starship operational maturity, refueling, lunar HLS variant — all multi-billion-dollar dependencies.

By 2030: Artemis crewed lunar landings have happened; lunar surface activity is starting; lunar base architecture is being built.

### Mars activity

Beyond Artemis lunar, Mars activity:

- **NASA Perseverance and Curiosity rovers** continue robotic exploration.
- **Sample return** mission (collaboration NASA-ESA) gets samples back.
- **SpaceX cargo Starships** target Mars cargo by late 2020s / early 2030s.
- **Crewed Mars missions** remain late-2030s+ aspiration.

Mars activity is real but constrained by transit windows (~every 2 years), budgets, and technology readiness. By 2030: significant robotic Mars activity continues; SpaceX cargo possibly demonstrating; crewed Mars still aspirational.

### In-space economy

Beyond launch, the space economy is growing:

- **Manufacturing in space**: pharmaceuticals, fiber optics, exotic materials. Several companies exploring.
- **Space stations**: ISS deorbits in late 2020s; commercial stations (Axiom, Vast, others) succeed it.
- **Lunar resources**: helium-3, water ice, regolith for construction.
- **Asteroid mining**: long-term aspiration.
- **Satellite servicing**: refueling and repair of satellites in orbit.

These create new launch demand and new business models. The in-space economy depends heavily on low-cost launch — Starship if it delivers economic targets.

### Strategic dependencies

The whole launch and space landscape depends on:

- **Starship operational maturity**: drives mass cost-per-kg down.
- **Refueling demonstration**: enables lunar and Mars at scale.
- **High cadence sustained**: economic model requires it.
- **Geopolitical stability**: US-China dynamics, supply chains.
- **Capital availability**: launch and space are capex-heavy.

Multiple paths exist:

- **Optimistic**: Starship delivers fully, lunar base develops, Mars activity starts, costs collapse.
- **Moderate**: Starship works at reduced economics, lunar activities scale, Mars remains research-level.
- **Pessimistic**: Starship hits major setback, Mars vision recedes, launch costs stagnate.

The decade ahead will determine which path.

### What SpaceX needs to do

For SpaceX's full vision to materialize:

1. **Demonstrate Starship operational reliability** — both stages reusing.
2. **Demonstrate in-orbit refueling** — critical for missions.
3. **Land humans on Moon via Artemis** — major NASA milestone.
4. **Land cargo on Mars** — major SpaceX milestone.
5. **Achieve cost targets** — $/kg dramatically lower than Falcon 9.
6. **Scale Starship cadence** — sustain operations at high rate.
7. **Survive correlated risks** — financial, regulatory, geopolitical, technical.

Each is in progress. None are guaranteed. The next 5 years are decisive.

### What it means for everyone else

If Starship delivers, the launch landscape transforms:

- **Cost of access to space drops 10-50x**.
- **Many more satellite operators** become viable.
- **Lunar and Mars exploration accelerates**.
- **In-space manufacturing** becomes economic.
- **Tourism** scales (initially expensive, slowly accessible).

If Starship doesn't deliver fully:

- **Falcon 9 economics still revolutionary** compared to legacy.
- **New competitors** still emerge.
- **Lunar progress slower** but real.
- **Mars vision recedes** but doesn't die.

Either way, the next decade is more interesting than the last for space.

## Three real-world scenarios

**Scenario 1: New Glenn reaches full operational status.**
Blue Origin's New Glenn demonstrates reliable orbital launches with first-stage recovery. Captures ~20% of US commercial launch market. Powers Blue Origin's lunar lander (Blue Moon) for some Artemis missions. SpaceX still dominant but less so. Healthy competition.

**Scenario 2: A Starship setback delays Artemis.**
Starship has a major test failure (lost vehicle plus pad damage at Boca Chica). NASA delays Artemis 3 by 2+ years. SpaceX rebuilds, iterates, eventually delivers. The delay is recoverable but expensive. Schedule risks materialize. Pattern continues.

**Scenario 3: Chinese rocket scales rapidly.**
Long March 9 (super-heavy) and Chinese reusable rockets reach operational status by 2030. Chinese launch capacity exceeds US in volume. Chinese mega-constellation (Guowang) competes globally with Starlink. US-China space competition intensifies. Major geopolitical implications.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Treating SpaceX as permanent monopoly** — competition is real.
- **Underestimating Chinese capability** — they're scaling fast.
- **Overestimating Mars timeline** — multi-decade vision.
- **Ignoring constellation demand** — it sustains the industry.
- **Treating Starship as guaranteed** — major milestones still ahead.

## Summary

- **SpaceX likely dominant** through 2030 but with growing competition.
- **Blue Origin** scales New Glenn; BE-4 engines deploy widely.
- **Chinese rockets** scale to rival SpaceX in volume.
- **ULA Vulcan** retains government share; less competitive commercially.
- **Rocket Lab** grows medium-lift presence.
- **Mega-constellations** sustain launch demand industry-wide.
- **NASA Artemis** delivers crewed lunar landings.
- **Mars activity** progresses cautiously.
- **In-space economy** emerges.
- **Starship's success or failure** is the decade's biggest variable.

That wraps Module 5 and the course. Space launch is in the middle of a transformation as dramatic as the introduction of the moving assembly line was for manufacturing. SpaceX is the central actor but the landscape is broader than any single company.
